1. What is Glicko 2 and why do we use it?

Glicko 2 is a ranking system developed by Professor Mark Glickman as an improvement over the traditional Elo system. Unlike simple point systems, Glicko 2 considers three factors for each player:

Rating (R)Represents the player's estimated skill100 – 3500
Rating Deviation (RD)Measures the uncertainty about the rating. More games = lower RD = more accurate rating30 – 350
Volatility (σ)Indicates performance consistency. Low values = stable results; high values = unpredictable player0.02 – 0.2

Advantages over traditional systems:

  • ✅ Quickly adjusts to the player's real skill level (thanks to RD)
  • ✅ Fairly penalizes those who play against very different skill levels
  • ✅ Detects and corrects performance inconsistencies (volatility)
  • ✅ All history matters, but recent matches have more weight

2. System components

• Rating (R): A number that indicates your level. The higher, the better.

• RD (Rating Deviation): The lower, the more reliable your rating. After many matches, RD stabilizes at a low value (~30). If you stop playing, RD increases.

• Volatility (σ): Measures your consistency. If you always finish in similar positions, volatility is low. If you alternate first and last places, volatility rises.

3. Why are ALL matches ranked?

At KA ESPORTS, every match you play affects your rating. There are no "friendly" or "practice" matches that don't count. This is possible because Glicko 2 automatically adjusts to all skill levels:

BeginnerYour RD is high (350), so your first matches will have a moderate impact until the system fine‑tunes your real level.
Experienced playerYour RD is low (30‑100), so each match will accurately reflect small changes in your performance.
Playing against similarly skilled opponentsPoint distribution is balanced and fair.
There's an upset (beginner beats master)The system recognizes that something unusual happened and adjusts ratings significantly, but controlled by volatility.

Advantages of this approach:

  • • Every match matters, encouraging constant participation.
  • • The system quickly adapts to your level, without needing separate categories.
  • • It's impossible to "inflate" your rating by only playing against beginners, because gains will be minimal.
  • • The entire community competes in the same ranking, from novices to legends.

4. Race scoring

Each 4‑player match distributes 96 total points as follows:

PositionRace pointsPlace points (for reference)
1st12🥇 12
2nd8🥈 8
3rd4🥉 4
4th04️⃣ 0

Important: Race points are not the rating. They are the input to the Glicko 2 algorithm. The rating is calculated after each match by comparing:

• Your result (race points)
• Your opponents' results
• The rating and RD of all players before the match

This means that finishing first is not enough: if you beat weak opponents, you'll gain few points; if you finish last but against very strong opponents, you'll lose few points.

8. Inactivity system (decay)

To keep the ranking up to date and reflect players' current skill levels, we apply two types of inactivity adjustments:

1. Uncertainty increase (RD) – Applies to EVERYONE

Every 7 days without playing (INACTIVITY_PERIOD_DAYS), the player's RD increases according to the formula:

φ' = √(φ² + σ²)

This means the rating becomes less reliable until the player competes again. It's a neutral measure: it does not penalize rating, only acknowledges we lack recent information.

2. Rating loss – Only for high‑level players

If a player has rating ≥ 1800 (RATING_DECAY_THRESHOLD) and remains inactive for 14 days (RATING_DECAY_DAYS), they will lose 5 points (RATING_DECAY_AMOUNT) for each complete inactivity period.

Why only for high ratings?
Because elite players must constantly demonstrate their level. If they stop competing, it's reasonable to assume their skill might have slightly decreased.

Practical example:

Player B190021 daysIncreases (3 periods)-10 points (2 periods of 14 days)

Both records are kept in the AUDIT_LOG for complete transparency.

10. Frequently asked questions

❔ Why does my rating change even if I finish last?

Because it also depends on your opponents' ratings. Losing to a much stronger opponent costs few points, while losing to a weaker one costs more. Example: If you have rating 2000 and lose to a player rated 1000, you will lose quite a few points (the system expected you to win easily). If you have 1000 and lose to a 2000, you will barely lose points (it was expected).

❔ How long do I need to be inactive for my rating to decay?

• RD starts increasing after 7 days.
• Rating starts losing points after 14 days only if your rating is ≥ 1800.

❔ What does "Unranked" mean?

It appears when you haven't reached the minimum matches for ranking: 5 matches for monthly rankings, 10 matches for global ranking. Once you meet the minimum, you'll get a rank based on your current rating.

❔ Can I see my previous matches?

Yes, in the MATCH_REPORTS_YYYY_MM sheets you'll find details of each match of the month, with rating changes and statistics.

❔ How is monthly SOS calculated?

Monthly SOS is the average of the pre‑match ratings of all opponents you faced during that month. A high value means you played against strong opponents.

❔ Why does RD sometimes increase and sometimes not?

RD only increases during inactivity. If you play regularly, RD stays low or even decreases (as the system gains confidence in your level).

❔ What does volatility mean?

It's a measure of your consistency. If you win and lose against similarly skilled opponents, your volatility will be low. If you alternate very different results (e.g., 1st and 4th constantly), volatility will increase.

❔ Are rating changes public?

Yes, all sheets are visible to the community. You can check the ranking, statistics, and match reports at any time.

❔ What should I do if I detect an error in my data?

Contact the administrators. The system maintains a detailed log of all operations (AUDIT_LOG) and we can review what happened.

11. Step by step calculation example

Let's simulate a match with four players. We'll use simplified values to convey the idea, although the real algorithm has additional steps (volatility calculation, iterations, etc.).

Initial data

PlayerRating (R)RD (φ)Volatility (σ)
A1500500.06
B14001000.06
C13001500.06
D12002000.06

Race results:

PlayerPoints
A40
B30

D6

Step 1: Convert to internal scale

Glicko 2 uses a different scale. It applies:

μ = (R - 1500) / 173.7178

φ = RD / 173.7178

But for this example, we'll keep the original values and focus on the logic.

Step 2: Calculate expected outcome for each pairing

For each pair of players, the probability that player i beats player j is calculated using:

E = 1 / (1 + exp(-g(φ_j) * (μ_i - μ_j)))

where g(φ) = 1 / sqrt(1 + 3φ²/π²).

This is complex, but the idea is that the greater the rating difference, the more likely the higher rated player will win.

Step 3: Adjust rating and RD

After the match, ratings are updated using Glicko 2 formulas. The rating change is proportional to the difference between the actual result (1, 0.5, 0) and the expected result, weighted by the opponent's RD.

Simplified result (for this example):

PlayerRating beforeChangeRating after
A1500+101510
B1400+51405
C1300-21298
D1200-151185

Notice that A gained many points because he beat lower rated opponents, but not as many as if he had beaten stronger rivals.

D lost many points because he finished last against higher rated opponents, which was expected, but the defeat was clear and his RD was high, amplifying the changes.

Step 4: Update RD and volatility

Each player's RD is reduced because they played (the system has more information). Volatility is adjusted according to the difference between the result and the expectation.
